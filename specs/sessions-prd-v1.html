Adaptive Daily Sessions

The core learning loop of Catalyst. Every day, every student gets a session built specifically for them — targeting their weak spots, introducing new concepts at the right time, and reinforcing what they've already learned before it fades. This document is the single source of truth for the entire sessions feature: what it does, why it works, and how to build it.

The one sentence

Every morning, Catalyst tells you exactly what to study today — and it's different from yesterday because you are different from yesterday.

Context

The Problem We're Solving

We talked to students preparing for CAT, GMAT, and CS university exams. The same three things came up in every conversation.

"I open my notes or a question bank and I don't know where to start. I usually just pick something random, or I practice stuff I already know because it feels productive."
— CS student, semester 3 exam prep
"I studied probability for two weeks, got good at it, then came back a month later for the mock test and it was like I'd never seen it. Gone."
— CAT aspirant, 6 months to exam
"Every app just gives me questions. None of them tell me if I'm actually improving or why I'm getting things wrong."
— GMAT prep, 3 months to test date

These aren't edge cases. They are the experience of the majority of self-studying students. The problems break down to three root causes:

Problem 1
No direction
Students waste decision energy choosing what to study. Most default to easy topics they already know — the illusion of productivity.
↓
Problem 2
No memory
Knowledge decays. The forgetting curve is real. Without scheduled review, 70% of what a student learned last week is gone by next week. No app they use fights this.
↓
Problem 3
No feedback loop
Students know their score but not their pattern. "73% accuracy" tells you nothing. "You consistently confuse preemptive and non-preemptive scheduling" tells you everything.
The incumbent alternative

Coaching classes solve problem 1 (a teacher tells you what to study) but fail on 2 and 3. Question banks solve nothing — they're libraries, not systems. Catalyst is the first product built to solve all three simultaneously, at a cost that doesn't require ₹2 lakh in fees.

Product Vision

What Sessions Sets Out to Achieve

The daily session is the heartbeat of the product. Everything else — the roadmap, the explanation panel, the enrichment pipeline — exists to make this single daily moment as valuable as possible.

The 5-minute rule

A session must deliver genuine learning value in 5 minutes. Not a warmup. Not a review of easy questions. Actual cognitive challenge in the student's zone of proximal development — the zone where learning actually happens. If it takes longer, great. But 5 minutes must feel worth it.

The system knows you

After 3 sessions, the system should feel like it understands the student's specific weaknesses. After 10 sessions, it should feel like a tutor that's been watching them study. This is the moat — not the technology, but the accumulated understanding of each individual student that gets harder to replicate over time.

Duolingo's lesson, not Duolingo's product

We drew inspiration from Duolingo's love of teaching and their streak mechanics — the habit loop that builds a daily practice. But we're not making a game. We're making a system that a serious exam aspirant would respect. The engagement mechanics exist to serve learning, not the other way around.

Users

Who This Feature Is For

Primary: Competitive exam aspirants

CAT and GMAT students with a real exam date. They have a deadline. They have high willingness to pay. They make their own decisions. A feature that tells them "here's exactly what to work on today to be ready by November" speaks directly to their anxiety and converts it into productive action.

Secondary: University CS students

Operating Systems, Data Structures, DBMS. Semester exams in 6–8 weeks. Less pressure than competitive exams but the same cognitive problems — no direction, no retention, no feedback loop. The daily session serves them identically because the underlying mechanics (ZPD, spaced repetition, accuracy tracking) are subject-agnostic.

Explicitly not a student who:

— wants passive content (video lectures, reading material)
— is looking for a social learning experience
— needs to be motivated by gamification before engaging
The student Catalyst serves already wants to study. They just need to know what and how.

Feature Design

The Full Sessions Flow

End-to-end user journey from app open to adaptive session, built on top of the existing roadmap infrastructure.

Onboarding (one-time)
1. Roadmap Creation
Student selects subject + topics + goal + exam date. Diagnostic calibration (3–5 MCQs). Roadmap generated via Qdrant semantic search + LLM block ordering. Stored as roadmaps.generated_json. This is the student's learning contract.
↓ (next day and every day after)
Daily — first app open
2. Today Widget Loads
GET /api/sessions/today — checks daily_sessions cache. If no session for today, triggers DailySessionGenerator: accuracy classifier → LLM session plan → targeted Qdrant searches per focus area → persisted to DB. Returns widget payload in ~3s on first open, <10ms on repeat.
↓
Student action
3. Student Reviews Widget
Sees today's subject, headline, estimated time, weekly progress, and up to 3 focus areas with their types (weakness / new / review). Taps "Begin Session."
↓
Session screen
4. Practice Session
MCQ questions served grouped by focus area. Per-question timer (green <60s, yellow <120s, red ≥120s). Prev/Skip/Next/See Results navigation. Single-tap locks the answer immediately — no edit, no confirmation. Explanation panel renders on every answer.
↓
Per question
5. Explanation Panel
State A (correct): green header, principle-first explanation, optional distractor note. State B (incorrect): red header, explanation of what's actually right, distractor note explaining why their choice was tempting. Bookmark toggle. Next button with 300ms debounce.
↓
End of session
6. Submit + Analysis
POST /api/sessions/{id}/submit with nested attempts grouped by focus area. Server recomputes correctness from stored payload — never trusts client. Returns per-topic summary with updated_state. Analysis screen shows what improved, what didn't, and a coaching note generated by LLM from real data.
↓ (tomorrow)
The loop closes
7. Tomorrow's Session Adapts
Redis accuracy cache invalidated on submission. Next day's DailySessionGenerator call reads the updated answer history. If the student improved on CPU Scheduling, it appears as "review" next time. If they struggled, it stays "weakness" with harder questions. The system has learned.

Post-Session Analysis
Engineering

System Architecture

The sessions system is built in four independent layers. Each layer can be understood, tested, and deployed separately. The interface between layers is always a function call or a DB read — never a side effect.

Layer 1 · Data
Per-topic accuracy classifier
get_topic_accuracy(user_id, roadmap_id) — joins answers → questions, computes accuracy per topic, classifies into new / weakness / review / mastered. Redis-cached, invalidated on every answer submission. Zero N+1. 18 tests passing.
↓ feeds
Layer 2 · Generation
DailySessionGenerator
One LLM call (Groq, ~300 tokens in/~200 out) to determine focus areas + copy. Then one targeted Qdrant semantic search per focus area using "Subject: X. Topic: Y. Difficulty: Z" query — reusing the same embedding pipeline as roadmap generation. Excludes questions answered in last 14 days. Persists full payload to daily_sessions table. 51 tests passing.
↓ stored in
Layer 3 · Cache
daily_sessions table
One row per (user, roadmap, date). Unique constraint prevents duplicate generation on concurrent requests. GET /api/sessions/today reads from here — sub-10ms on all repeat opens within the same day. Generation only happens once per student per roadmap per day.
↓ served by
Layer 4 · API
Today endpoint + Submit endpoint
GET /api/sessions/today reshapes the cached payload to the Android spec. POST /api/sessions/{id}/submit recomputes correctness server-side, writes SessionAttempt rows, runs topic state transitions, invalidates Redis, returns per-topic summary.
Key design principle

The expensive operations (Qdrant search, LLM call) happen exactly once per student per day, lazily on first app open. Every subsequent open reads from the DB cache. At 1,000 DAU, total LLM cost is under ₹50/day.

Engineering

Topic Accuracy Classifier

The four-state model that drives every session composition decision. Built, tested, and live.

Status: Done · 18 tests passing

practice/service/topicAccuracy.py — get_topic_accuracy(user_id, roadmap_id). Redis-first, exactly 2 DB queries, zero N+1. Invalidated from ProcessAttempts.py outside the atomic block so it only fires on successful commit.

State Condition Session behaviour Question count
new attempts < 3 (any accuracy) Introductory questions, lower difficulty, builds foundation 6
weakness accuracy < 65% AND attempts ≥ 3 Priority — always first focus area. Mixed difficulty targeting gaps 8
review accuracy ≥ 65% AND attempts ≥ 3 AND not mastered Spaced repetition — medium-hard questions to sharpen retention 5
mastered accuracy ≥ 80% AND attempts ≥ 5 Excluded from sessions. V2: one question every 7 days as confidence booster 0 (V1)
Why attempts < 3 = "new" regardless of accuracy

A student who answered 1 question wrong has 0% accuracy — but you can't confidently call that a weakness yet. Sparse data misleads the classifier. Requiring ≥ 3 attempts before weakness/review classification prevents the system from overreacting to a single bad answer and serving remedial content prematurely.

Engineering

DailySessionGenerator

Status: Done · 51 tests passing

roadmap/service/dailySessionGenerator.py — 5-step synchronous pipeline. Reuses the existing Qdrant infrastructure from roadmap generation.

Step 1
Cache check
If daily_sessions row exists for (user, roadmap, today) — return it immediately. Zero work on repeat calls. This is the path for 95%+ of app opens.
↓ (only on first open of the day)
Step 2
LLM session plan
One Groq call (~300 tokens in, ~200 out) with: roadmap subject, full topic list, per-topic accuracy + classifications from the accuracy classifier. Returns: up to 3 focus areas ranked by priority, question counts, topicHeadline, reason copy. Fractions of a cent per call.
↓
Step 3
Targeted Qdrant search per focus area
For each focus area, build query: "Subject: OS. Topic: CPU Scheduling. Difficulty: 3". Embed via HuggingFace Inference API (same all-MiniLM-L6-v2 model as roadmap generation). Cosine search in Qdrant. Filter in Python by difficulty (avoids Qdrant payload inconsistency from pre-migration string values). Exclude question IDs answered in last 14 days. One Qdrant search + one Postgres IN query per focus area — no N+1.
↓
Step 4
Compute derived fields
questionCount = sum of all focus area counts. estimatedMinutes = questionCount × 1.2 rounded. bloomsRange.min/max = MIN/MAX bloom_level across all fetched questions. weeklyProgress = COUNT sessions completed this week.
↓
Step 5
Persist to daily_sessions
Full payload written to daily_sessions table including the full question objects inside each focus area (not returned to client — used for server-side correctness recomputation on submit). Returns payload.
Known issue: duplicate question IDs (DS-007)

The generator can return the same question ID in multiple focus areas when topics share semantically similar questions in Qdrant. Fix: add deduplication step after all Qdrant searches complete — remove any question ID appearing more than once before persisting. Not yet implemented.

Cold-start fallback needed (DS-006)

Day-1 users with zero answer history return an empty session because the accuracy classifier has nothing to query. Fallback: when all topics classify as "new", seed the session from the canonical topic list for the subject at difficulty 1–2. Canonical OS list defined. GMAT/CAT canonical lists needed before those segments launch.

Engineering

Session Submission Logic

The submit endpoint closes the learning loop. Every decision here is intentional.

Server-side correctness recomputation

The client sends is_correct but the server ignores it. Correctness is always recomputed by loading the session's payload_json from daily_sessions and comparing selected_index against question.correct_index. This prevents any client-side manipulation of scores and ensures the adaptive engine always trains on accurate data.

Topic state transitions

The topic_type sent per focus area by the client (e.g. "weakness") is used as the previous_state input to the transition function — the client already knows the state because it came from the today endpoint. No UserTopicState table needed in V1.

Previous stateSession accuracyNew state
weakness≥ 80%mastered
weakness50–79%review
weakness< 50%weakness
new≥ 80%mastered
new50–79%review
new< 50%weakness
review≥ 80%mastered
review< 80%review

Idempotency

If daily_sessions.submitted_at is already set, return 409 {"status": "already_submitted"}. The Android app treats 409 as success — safe to retry on network failure.

Unknown question IDs

Return 422. Do not skip and log. Question IDs come from the server's own session payload — an unknown ID is a client bug or a tampered request, not an edge case to handle gracefully. Silently skipping corrupts the adaptive engine's accuracy signal.

SessionAttempt model

A new SessionAttempt table stores rich per-question data: time_to_first_tap_ms (capped at 120,000), sequence_position, bloom_level, answer_changed, and the recomputed is_correct. The existing Answer table is written to in parallel for analytics continuity — flagged for migration once the analytics pipeline is updated to read from SessionAttempt.

Decisions Log

Every significant architecture or product decision made during design and implementation. Recorded here so future team members understand why, not just what.

Lazy generation over Celery beat
Sessions are generated on first app open, not at midnight via a scheduled job. Reason: simpler infrastructure at current scale. No Celery beat to maintain, no missed jobs if a user doesn't open the app. When DAU reaches ~10,000 and the 3-second first-open lag becomes a product issue, switch to midnight pre-generation.
One LLM call per day, not per question
The session plan (focus areas, copy, question counts) comes from a single Groq call. Questions are fetched via Qdrant, not LLM. This keeps the hot path cheap: at 1,000 DAU, total LLM cost for sessions is under ₹50/day. Any LLM call per question per user would multiply this by 15–20x.
topic_type from client as previous_state
The submission endpoint uses the client's topic_type field as the previous state for transition logic rather than a UserTopicState table. Rationale: the client already knows the state (it came from the today endpoint), storing it server-side for V1 adds a table and migration for minimal benefit. Revisit if we need historical state tracking for analytics.
422 on unknown question IDs — reject, not skip
Question IDs originate from the server's own session payload. An unknown ID is always a bug or a tamper — not a valid edge case. Skipping corrupts the accuracy classifier's training data. Rejected.
Difficulty filtered in Python, not Qdrant
Pre-migration, Qdrant stored difficulty as strings ("easy"/"medium"/"hard"). Post-migration they're integers 1–5. Filtering in Python avoids Qdrant payload inconsistency for questions embedded before the migration. Fix Qdrant payload values when the question bank is re-embedded and move filter back to Qdrant query.
roadmap_question table left untouched (intentional debt)
The roadmap_question join table exists but nothing writes to it — roadmaps are stored as generated_json blobs. The daily sessions layer was built on top of raw answer history rather than requiring a proper per-question mastery state. This is conscious technical debt, documented here, to be addressed in V2 when the July cohort's data informs a proper FSRS migration.
